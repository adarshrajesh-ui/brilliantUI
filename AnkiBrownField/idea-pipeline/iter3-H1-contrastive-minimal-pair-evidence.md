# Iteration 3 · H1 — Ethics Contrastive Minimal-Pair Deck: Evidence Dossier

> Blunt, evidence-first dossier. Goal H1 of Iteration 3: decide whether the SPECIFIC
> "contrastive minimal-pair / comparing near-miss cases" mechanism has its own independent
> A/B evidence, distinct from the interleaving evidence it already rides (Rohrer 2015 d=0.79;
> Brunmair 2019 g=0.42), OR whether it is just interleaving relabeled.
> Format per finding: **VERBATIM quote (author, year, locator)** → methodology summary → **GRADE (A–D)**.
> Grade key: **A** = meta / large RCT, adult+applied, effect+CI, replicated, ecologically valid ·
> **B** = solid design + replication but lab-leaning or proxy population/task ·
> **C** = single paradigm / small / undergrad-lab / correlational / essay-only · **D** = anecdote / unreplicated.
> Nothing fabricated; unsourced claims marked **UNVERIFIED**.

## TOPIC 1 — Contrasting cases / "a time for telling" (Schwartz)

### Finding 1-1 — Schwartz & Bransford 1998, "A Time for Telling" (the founding paper)
> "In three experiments, students analyzed contrasting cases that brought to light memory phenomena. Afterward, they received a lecture or text on memory. Approximately 1 week later they predicted outcomes for a hypothetical experiment … The students made more accurate predictions compared to the control treatments of (a) reading about the features in the cases and hearing a lecture, (b) summarizing a relevant text and hearing a lecture, and (c) analyzing the cases twice without receiving a lecture. … analyzing the contrasting cases provided students with the differentiated knowledge structures necessary to understand a subsequent explanation at a deep level."
> — **Schwartz & Bransford, 1998**, *Cognition and Instruction*, 16(4), 475–522, abstract (doi:10.1207/s1532690xci1604_4).

**Methodology:** Three experiments, undergraduates, within- and between-subjects. Students either *analyzed/graphed contrasting datasets* (near-miss experimental cases) or got matched control activities (reading summaries, text summarizing, or double case-analysis with no lecture), then a lecture, then a ~1-week-delayed novel-prediction test. Contrasting-case analysis + subsequent telling beat every control on applying concepts to novel problems; fact-recall was equal across conditions.
**GRADE: B.** Seminal, replicated across 3 experiments, delayed transfer test, real conceptual content — but undergrad lab, small N per cell, and the "case analysis" is an instructor-guided graphing task, not a self-serve flashcard. Establishes the *mechanism* (differentiation before telling), not app-level magnitude.

### Finding 1-2 — Schwartz, Chase, Oppezzo & Chin 2011, "Practicing versus inventing with contrasting cases" (the RCT with an effect size)
> "With respect to posttest performance, the ICC students exhibited greater transfer; F(1, 111) = 10.6, MSE = 0.20, p = .002, d = .31 … For the word problems, there were no treatment differences, F(1, 111) = 0.21, MSE = 0.03, p = .65, d = .04."
> — **Schwartz, Chase, Oppezzo & Chin, 2011**, *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 103(4), 759–775, Exp. 1 Results (doi:10.1037/a0025140).

**Methodology:** Two controlled classroom experiments teaching physics (density, etc.) to adolescents. Exp. 1 (N=128): "tell-and-practice" (told the formula, then practiced on contrasting cases) vs. "invent-with-contrasting-cases" (invent a measure from the same cases, told only afterward). Exp. 2 (N=120) isolated sources of the effect. Both groups equal on routine word problems; ICC produced better *deep-structure transfer* to semantically unrelated ratio topics.
**GRADE: B+.** Randomized, adequately powered, real curriculum, a clean transfer/near dissociation, replicated in a second experiment, benefits both low- and high-achievers. Held below A: adolescents (not adults), transfer effect is *modest* (d=0.31), and the "invent" framing is more than bare comparison.

**Blunt read of Topic 1:** Contrasting cases reliably improve *deep-structure/transfer* performance while leaving *surface/routine* performance unchanged — exactly the profile you want for "which Standard, and why" discrimination, but the transfer gains are moderate (d≈0.3), not spectacular.

## TOPIC 2 — Analogical comparison / structural alignment for category learning & transfer

### Finding 2-1 — Alfieri, Nokes-Malach & Schunn 2013 — the meta-analysis (headline pillar)
> "we conducted a meta-analysis of 57 experiments with 336 tests. Random effects analyses of the 336 tests revealed that case comparison activities commonly led to greater learning outcomes than other forms of case study including sequential, single case, and nonanalogous, as well as traditional instruction and control (d = .50), 95% CI [.44, .56]. Of 15 potential moderators, four … reliably moderate[d] the effectiveness … the objective of the comparison, the presentation of a principle, the content, and the lag between the comparison and testing. Asking learners to find similarities between cases, providing principles after the comparisons, using perceptual content, and testing learners immediately are all associated with greater learning."
> — **Alfieri, Nokes-Malach & Schunn, 2013**, *Educational Psychologist*, 48(2), 87–113, abstract (doi:10.1080/00461520.2013.775712).

**Methodology:** Random-effects meta-analysis, 57 experiments / 336 effect-size tests, spanning lab and classroom, comparing *case comparison* against sequential study, single-case study, non-analogous cases, and traditional instruction/control. Overall d=0.50 [.44, .56]; moderator analyses on 15 variables.
**GRADE: A−.** Large, pre-registered-style moderator meta with tight CI, exactly the manipulation of interest (comparison vs. sequential/single). Held a notch below A only because the corpus is heterogeneous (mixed ages, many lab tasks) and effect is medium. **This is the load-bearing pillar and it is genuinely independent of the interleaving literature** (it contrasts *co-presented comparison* vs. sequential/single, not *spaced/interleaved schedule*).

**Design-critical moderators (blunt):** the benefit is *conditional* — (a) prompt learners to find **similarities/differences** (don't just show pairs), (b) **present the governing principle AFTER** the comparison (i.e., reveal "Standard III(C) + the decisive fact" post-attempt), (c) it was strongest with **perceptual content and immediate testing**. The last point is a real tension with spaced-repetition delivery — see the verdict caveats.

### Finding 2-2 — Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson 2003 — analogical encoding in ADULTS/professionals (applied pillar)
> "participants in the comparison condition were over twice as likely (48% of participants) to transfer the principle to the test case as participants in the separate-cases condition (19%), χ²(1, N = 128) = 11.85, p < .01."
> — **Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson, 2003**, *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 95(2), 393–408, Exp. 2 Results (doi:10.1037/0022-0663.95.2.393).
> "Participants who compared the two cases and wrote out the commonalities were nearly three times as likely to use the negotiation strategies in a subsequent face-to-face negotiation as those who analyzed the same cases one at a time … We have replicated this finding of a two- or three-fold advantage … using … several groups of advanced learners (e.g., accountants, consultants, managers, and executives)." — *ibid.*, Introduction (summarizing Thompson, Gentner & Loewenstein 2000; Loewenstein, Thompson & Gentner 1999).

**Methodology:** Three studies teaching negotiation principles (trade-offs, contingent contracts). The clean isolation is Exp. 2 (N=128 undergrads): *the same two cases* given to compare vs. study separately — comparison 48% vs separate 19% transfer to a novel surface-different problem. Exp. 3 extended to a live face-to-face negotiation (70% comparison vs 55% separate vs 37% no-cases). Prior papers replicate the 2–3× effect in MBAs and working professionals.
**GRADE: A−.** Multiple experiments, a *content-matched* comparison-vs-separate contrast (rules out "more material"), transfer to a genuinely novel and even face-to-face task, and — crucially for our rubric — **replicated in adult professionals (MBAs, accountants, consultants, managers, executives)**. The single best ecological-validity match for a professional-exam population.

### Finding 2-3 — Gick & Holyoak 1983 — schema induction from two analogs (the classic mechanism + the caveat)
> "the experiments in Part II demonstrated that if two prior analogs were given, subjects often derived a problem schema as an incidental product of describing the similarities of the analogs. The quality of the induced schema was highly predictive of subsequent transfer performance. … However, only about 40% of the subjects described the similarities between the two analogs in terms that approximated the convergence schema."
> — **Gick & Holyoak, 1983**, *Cognitive Psychology*, 15(1), 1–38, abstract & Results (doi:10.1016/0010-0285(83)90002-6).

**Methodology:** Series of experiments on Duncker's radiation problem. One story analog → ~30% spontaneous convergence solutions (vs ~10% no-analog baseline); a single analog with summaries/principle/diagram largely *failed* to boost transfer, but **two analogs** compared for similarity produced schema induction that predicted transfer.
**GRADE: B.** Foundational, heavily cited, establishes that *comparing two* cases (not one) is what induces the transferable schema — the theoretical spine of minimal-pair design. Downgraded: undergrad puzzle task, and it doubles as a **warning** — even with two analogs only ~40% spontaneously abstract the schema, and spontaneous *noticing/transfer* frequently fails without an explicit prompt. Implication: the deck must *force* the contrast, not hope for it.

### Finding 2-4 — Rittle-Johnson & Star 2007 — comparison vs. sequential, classroom RCT (independent replication in a rule-application domain)
> "the authors randomly assigned 70 seventh-grade students to learn about algebra equation solving by either (a) comparing and contrasting alternative solution methods or (b) reflecting on the same solution methods one at a time. At posttest, students in the compare group had made greater gains in procedural knowledge and flexibility and comparable gains in conceptual knowledge."
> — **Rittle-Johnson & Star, 2007**, *Journal of Educational Psychology*, 99(3), 561–574, abstract (doi:10.1037/0022-0663.99.3.561).

**Methodology:** Randomized classroom experiment, N=70, pre/post. Both groups saw the *same* worked examples in *pairs on a page*; only the compare group was cued to compare/contrast the two, the control reflected on them one at a time. Compare group gained more procedural knowledge + flexibility (choosing efficient methods), equal conceptual gains.
**GRADE: B/B+.** Randomized, content-matched (same examples, only the compare cue differs → clean isolation of the comparison mechanism), rule-application domain (multi-step procedure selection) that rhymes with "which Standard applies." Held below A: small N, 7th-graders, single study (though the Rittle-Johnson/Star program replicated it repeatedly afterward).

**Blunt read of Topic 2:** ≥3 independent research programs (Alfieri meta, Gentner/negotiation, Gick&Holyoak, Rittle-Johnson/Star) converge that **co-presented comparison beats sequential/separate study of the same material** for schema abstraction and transfer. This is the strongest leg of the feature and it is *mechanistically distinct from interleaving* (simultaneous alignment vs. temporal schedule).

## TOPIC 3 — Minimal-pair / contrastive discrimination of CONFUSABLE categories

### Finding 3-1 — Corral, Kurtz & Jones 2020 — between-category CONTRAST beats within-category comparison for discriminating confusable categories
> "Subjects in the contrast condition outperformed those in the match condition for feature-based categories (Experiment 1) and across 4 different types of relational categories (Experiments 1–4). … feature-based theories predict a benefit of between-category comparisons, because such comparisons direct attention to the features that discriminate the categories."
> — **Corral, Kurtz & Jones, 2020**, *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General*, "Learning relational concepts from within- versus between-category comparisons," abstract (doi:10.1037/xge0000517).
> "a two-way ANOVA revealed a main effect of comparison on one-item trials, F(1, 167) = 5.26, p = .023, MSE = .038. … subjects in the contrast conditions achieved higher accuracy than subjects in the match conditions, for feature-based categories and also for relational categories." — *ibid.*, Exp. 1 Results.

**Methodology:** Adults; two-category classification learning where each trial co-presents two items either from the *same* category (match) or *opposing* categories (contrast). Tested feature-based and 4 relational category types across 4 experiments (Exp. 1 N≈169). Discrimination measured on identical one-item probe trials (controls for task difficulty). **Between-category contrast (the "minimal pair") reliably beat same-category comparison.**
**GRADE: B.** The single most *on-mechanism* study for our feature: it directly tests whether juxtaposing near-miss items from *opposing* categories improves the ability to *discriminate confusable categories* — and it does, for both feature and relational categories, replicated across 4 experiments in adults. Downgraded: abstract geometric/relational stimuli in a lab, not ethics vignettes; magnitude reported as a main effect, not a clean d.

**Note on the "minimal pair" framing:** the classic *phonetic* minimal-pair / high-variability training literature (e.g., non-native /r/–/l/ discrimination) also supports contrastive discrimination, but I did **not** verify a specific effect-size-bearing quote for it in this pass → treat perceptual-minimal-pair support as **UNVERIFIED here** and lean on Corral/Kurtz for the discrimination claim.

## TOPIC 4 — Teaching professional ETHICS / legal rule-application via contrasting scenarios

### Finding 4-1 — Legal-ethics pedagogy explicitly rejects single pre-digested cases in favor of ambiguous, comparative problem scenarios
> "both teaching and assessment strategies must avoid reliance on 'predigested' or already interpreted fact scenarios, such as appellate decisions or casebook problems that identify the conduct rule to be applied … To develop more mature moral reasoning, students must struggle with complex problems in which the protagonist is a lawyer facing competing duties … that cannot be resolved by application of a rule."
> — **Cunningham & Alexander**, "Developing Professional Judgment," book chapter (teachinglegalethics.com), pp. 8–9; and **"Learning Professional Responsibility for the Practice of Law"** (2015), p. ~4.
> "significantly increased DIT [Defining Issues Test] scores are produced by the use of small group dilemma discussions that require students to present criteria for well-reasoned arguments … In 33 studies of the effects of professional education, none showed significant increases in DIT scores without a carefully validated ethics curriculum." — *Cunningham & Alexander*, citing Bebeau.

**Methodology:** These are pedagogical scholarship / program descriptions + reference to Bebeau's dental-ethics DIT intervention studies (pre/post DIT gains) and a synthesis of 33 professional-education studies. They argue for contrasting, ambiguous, near-miss scenarios over rule-labeled appellate cases, and report that validated ethics curricula raise moral-reasoning (DIT) scores.
**GRADE: C (and partly UNVERIFIED for our specific claim).** This is directional, expert-consensus, and DIT-outcome evidence — NOT a controlled A/B test of *contrastive minimal-pair vignettes* against single vignettes for *rule/Standard discrimination*. Bebeau's DIT gains are real but measure moral-sensitivity/reasoning development, not "picks the right Standard on a near-miss." **No study located that A/B-tests contrastive minimal pairs on professional-ethics rule application specifically.**

**Blunt read of Topic 4:** The domain-specific evidence (ethics/legal rule application) is the **weakest link**. Support for the ethics application is *transferred by analogy* from math, physics, negotiation, and category learning — which is legitimate (the mechanism is domain-general) but must be stated honestly: nobody has run the exact experiment on CFA-style Standards.

## IS IT JUST INTERLEAVING RELABELED? — No.

- **Interleaving** (Rohrer 2015, Brunmair 2019) manipulates the **temporal schedule** of practice: mix problem *types* across a session so retrieval must first *select* the right approach. The items are seen *one at a time, spread out*.
- **Contrastive minimal-pair comparison** manipulates **simultaneous co-presentation + explicit alignment**: two near-miss cases side by side, learner forced to name the *one decisive difference*. The mechanism is *structural alignment / diagnostic-feature discovery* (Gentner; Corral/Kurtz), not spacing.
- The cleanest proof they're different: Gentner Exp 2 and Rittle-Johnson & Star both hold the **exact same items constant** and vary *only* compare-vs-separate — and comparison still wins (48% vs 19%; procedural gains). That effect cannot be a spacing/interleaving effect because the schedule is identical.
- **They are complementary, not redundant:** interleave the confusable-Standard clusters across the session (Rohrer) AND co-present the near-miss pair with a forced "what's the decisive fact?" prompt (Gentner/Alfieri/Corral). Two mechanisms, two evidence bases.

## BUILDABILITY SANITY CHECK (one paragraph)

Realistic — and cheap *precisely because Diamond #1 already exists*. The deck is the **same machine** pointed at ethics: a curated, human/CFA-verified bank of ~150–250 vignette *pairs* (offline LLM drafting + human verification, exactly the Diamond #1 workflow — **no runtime LLM**), a custom note type (`CFA Ethics Minimal-Pair` with fields: shared stem, Variant-A facts, Variant-B decisive fact, correct verdict A, correct verdict B, Standard label, decisive-difference explanation), and **fully deterministic scoring** (two binary conform/violate calls + a which-Standard multiple-choice against the 3 confusable clusters). No new grading model, no free-response, no far-transfer measurement. It reuses the `los::x::y` tagging + sibling-deck pattern and can reuse the existing queue (interleave the 3 clusters) with **zero new Rust** required (a nice-to-have would be a "serve both variants of a pair in one session" ordering hint, a small scheduler tweak, not a blocker). The only real cost is *authoring* the pairs and clusters — genuine subject-matter work, but bounded, and directly analogous to the item-bank effort already budgeted for #1.

## BLUNT VERDICT

**It SATURATES as an independent Diamond #2 — with one honest asterisk.**

- **Evidence: STRONGLY grounded (STRONGER than the interleaving base it was riding).** ≥5 independent A/B pillars, two at A−, spanning a meta-analysis (d=0.50 [.44,.56]), adult-professional applied studies (Gentner, 2–3× transfer), and the exactly-on-point "contrast beats comparison for discriminating confusable categories" result (Corral/Kurtz). It is **not** interleaving relabeled — the content-matched compare-vs-separate studies prove a distinct, simultaneous-alignment mechanism.
- **Buildable: YES**, deterministically, on the existing curated-bank + note-type infrastructure, with essentially no new engine risk.
- **Distinct from Diamond #1: MECHANISTICALLY yes** (structural alignment / diagnostic discrimination ≠ retrieval-practice + spacing + memory-vs-performance). **Infrastructurally no** — it is the same authoring/note-type/deterministic-scoring machine, which is exactly why it is cheap. So it is a *distinct-mechanism diamond that shares #1's toolchain*, not a sub-feature of #1's retrieval engine.
- **The asterisk (do not hide it):** the *ethics/legal-rule-application-specific* evidence is **C/UNVERIFIED** — the support is transferred-by-analogy from math, physics, negotiation, and category learning. The mechanism is domain-general and the transfer is reasonable, but nobody has A/B-tested contrastive minimal pairs on professional-ethics Standard discrimination. Ship it as an evidence-grounded *mechanism*, not as "proven for CFA ethics."
- **Non-negotiable design constraints from the evidence:** (1) **force the comparison** — require the learner to state the single decisive fact (Gick & Holyoak: unprompted comparison/transfer often fails; only ~40% abstract the schema spontaneously). (2) **Reveal the governing principle AFTER** the attempt (Alfieri moderator). (3) Expect gains on **discrimination/transfer**, not on rote surface performance (Schwartz d≈0.31; word-problem d≈0.04). (4) Note the **immediate-testing moderator** (Alfieri): the comparison benefit was strongest at immediate test — mitigate by keeping the *pair* co-present within a single card/session rather than splitting variants across far-spaced reviews.

**Recommended ledger action:** promote "Ethics contrastive minimal-pair deck" from 7.5–8.0 candidate to **DIAMOND #2 (~8.5)**, evidence leg RESOLVED (≥5 independent A/B pillars, distinct mechanism), buildability GO on existing infra, with the ethics-domain-specificity caveat recorded as the only remaining (non-load-bearing) weakness.
