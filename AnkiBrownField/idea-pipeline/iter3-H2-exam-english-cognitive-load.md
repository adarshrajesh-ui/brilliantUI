# Iteration 3 · H2 — Vet the "Exam-English / Extraneous-Cognitive-Load" axis as an INDEPENDENT Diamond #3 candidate

**Mandate:** Decide whether an "exam-English layer" for non-native English (L2) CFA candidates is a *genuinely orthogonal* diamond (a language-processing-load axis, distinct from Diamond #1's retrieval/transfer/memory axis) that is (a) grounded in A/B evidence, (b) buildable, and (c) a big enough draw. Blunt, evidence-first. Never fabricate; mark UNVERIFIED where needed.

**BLUF (blunt):** The **axis is genuinely orthogonal** to Diamond #1 (this is working-memory load during *reading/parsing*, not memory durability or far-transfer) and the **market is real and large** (Asia Pacific ≈ 54–59% of all CFA exam-takers). **BUT the headline claim — "reducing extraneous *language* load measurably improves non-native exam performance" — is plausible-but-thin, not backed by ≥3 independent A/B pillars pointing the same way.** The single strongest *direct* meta-analytic test (linguistic **simplification** of test items) is **essentially NULL** (g≈0.03, ns; Kieffer 2009). What *does* survive meta-analysis is narrower and small: **vocabulary/glossary support** (~0.15 SD) and **extra time** (time-pressure interaction). Verdict: **WEAK** — orthogonal and big-market, but the load-bearing intervention evidence is thin and mostly on children, not adults under high-stakes time pressure.

## TASK A — EVIDENCE (graded)

### Pillar 1 — Cognitive Load Theory: extraneous vs intrinsic load; reducing extraneous load improves learning

**VERBATIM:** "A combination of high intrinsic and high extraneous cognitive load may be fatal to learning because working memory may be substantially exceeded … [I]t may be essential to design instruction in a manner that reduces extraneous cognitive load."
— Sweller, van Merriënboer & Paas (1998), *Educational Psychology Review*, pp. 263–264 (quoted in NSW CESE 2017 review).

**VERBATIM (recent formulation):** "Recently, empirical studies have found that when extraneous cognitive load was reduced, the total cognitive load also decreased (Sweller et al., 2019) … instructional strategies and learning materials should optimize intrinsic load … while minimizing extraneous load as it impedes learning."
— PMC review of CLT applications (2024/2025), PMC12246501, §"Extraneous cognitive load."

**Methodology:** CLT is a 35-year theoretical framework with a large downstream experimental base. The canonical extraneous-load reductions (worked-example effect, split-attention effect, redundancy effect) are supported by many RCTs and by meta-analyses of the contiguity effects (e.g., spatial/temporal contiguity meta-analysis, Ginns 2006, *Learning & Instruction*). Populations range from schoolchildren to adult learners; tasks are typically math/science/technical problem-solving; outcomes are learning/transfer test scores and subjective load.

**GRADE: A** (for the *general* mechanism). Well-replicated theory, multiple RCTs + meta-analyses, effect sizes and mechanism robust.
**Caveat that caps its use here:** CLT is domain-general. It establishes that *extraneous* load hurts learning and that reducing it helps — but the CLT literature's "extraneous load" is usually *instructional-design* clutter (split attention, redundancy), **not linguistic/L2 parsing load specifically.** Using CLT to justify an "exam-English layer" is an *extrapolation*, not a direct test. A-tier for the mechanism, but it does **not** by itself establish the language-specific claim.

### Pillar 2 — Language load as construct-irrelevant variance in high-stakes testing for L2 test-takers (linguistic simplification of items)

**VERBATIM (seminal, positive but small):** "Linguistic modification of test items resulted in significant differences in math performance; scores on the linguistically modified version were slightly higher. Some student groups benefited more from the linguistic modification of items — in particular, students in low-level and average math classes, but also ELLs and low SES students."
— Abedi & Lord (2001), *Applied Measurement in Education*, 14(3), pp. 219–234, abstract.

**VERBATIM (the blunt meta-analytic counter):** "only one accommodation — providing English dictionaries or glossaries — has a statistically significant effect on ELLs' performance, and this effect equates to only a small reduction in the achievement score gap … accommodations to reduce the impact of limited language proficiency on academic skill assessment are **not particularly effective**."
— Kieffer, Lesaux, Rivera & Francis (2009), *Review of Educational Research*, 79(3), pp. 1168–1201, abstract. (In their tables: **Simplified English g = 0.030, ns (p=.479)**; English dictionary/glossary g = 0.146, p=.001.)

**VERBATIM (second meta, no support for simplification):** "Although previous studies have suggested that linguistic simplification may be more effective than other methods, results from this meta-analysis offered no support for that suggestion." … overall accommodation effect "about 0.157 standard deviations — a relatively small but statistically significant increase," with only English proficiency as a significant moderator (low-proficiency ELLs benefit more).
— Li & Suen (2012), *Applied Measurement in Education*, 25(4), abstract.

**Methodology:** Abedi & Lord = one field study, N=1,174 U.S. 8th graders, NAEP math items vs. linguistically-modified parallels, paper-and-pencil; ELLs scored lower than proficient speakers, modified items *slightly* higher. Kieffer 2009 = meta-analysis of 11–16 randomized/quasi-randomized studies of school-age ELLs across 7 accommodation types on large-scale math/science. Li & Suen 2012 = HLM meta-analysis of ELL accommodation studies. Pennock-Román & Rivera (2011, *EMIP* 30(3)) = meta of 14 U.S. studies (50 ELL groups, Glass's d), classified by accommodation × timing.

**GRADE:**
- Abedi & Lord 2001 → **B** (single field study, adolescents not adults, small effect, but seminal and directionally clean; construct-irrelevant-variance framing solid).
- Kieffer 2009 / Li & Suen 2012 / Pennock-Román & Rivera 2011 → **A− (meta, randomized primary studies)** — and they point the *opposite* way from the naive hypothesis: **item *simplification* is null; only vocabulary support (dictionary/glossary) is significant and small (~0.15 SD).**

**Net for this pillar (blunt):** the measurement literature is the most *direct* test of "reduce language load → better NNS scores," and it **largely fails to support sentence-level simplification.** It supports a narrower thing: **vocabulary access.** This is a genuine, adult-relevant, high-stakes-adjacent body — but it caps, rather than confirms, the headline.

### Pillar 3 — Pre-teaching domain vocabulary / reducing sentence complexity improves L2 test performance

**VERBATIM (vocabulary → comprehension meta):** "Vocabulary instruction was found to be effective at increasing students' ability to comprehend text with custom measures (d = 0.50), but was less effective for standardized measures (d = 0.10) … students with reading difficulties (d = 1.23) benefited more than three times as much as students without reading problems (d = 0.39)."
— Elleman, Lindo, Morphy & Compton (2009), *Journal of Research on Educational Effectiveness*, meta-analysis of 37 studies (grades pre-K–12), abstract.

**VERBATIM (glossary is the one thing that works, from Pillar 2 meta):** "the only significant positive effect was found with the English dictionary/glossary accommodation with an average effect of .229 (p < .05)" [extended-time condition]; "the only significant positive effect was found with the pop-up English glossary accommodation yielding an average effect size of .285 (p < .05)" [restricted-time condition].
— Pennock-Román & Rivera (2011), as summarized in Kachchaf et al. validity review (UMass, doi:10.7275/w1xv-3n96).

**Methodology:** Elleman 2009 = 37 vocabulary-intervention studies, comprehension outcomes split by measure type (researcher-designed "custom" vs. standardized). Pennock-Román/Kieffer = the glossary sub-effects from the accommodation metas above.

**GRADE: B / B− for the *application* claim.**
Reasons for the cap: (i) the meta effect is on **general/struggling readers and children, not adult L2 professionals**; (ii) the effect is **large on custom-aligned tests (d≈0.50) but collapses to non-significant on standardized tests (d=0.10)** — and the CFA exam behaves like a standardized test, not a teach-to-the-glossary test; (iii) "pre-teaching vocabulary → better score on an unrelated standardized item" is exactly the transfer that shrinks. **Vocabulary support has real, replicated, but SMALL and measure-dependent support.** "Reducing sentence complexity" specifically = **UNVERIFIED / contradicted at the meta level** (Kieffer simplified-English null; see Pillar 2).

### Pillar 4 — Reading speed / time-pressure interactions for L2 test-takers

**VERBATIM (L2 slower; needs ~25% more time):** "Studies of Swedish university students, who are advanced L2 users … have shown that they can reach similar levels of comprehension to L1 readers, although they need approximately 25% more time for it (Shaw & McMillion, 2008, 2011)." In this study's own data (N=295 undergraduates), the L2 parallel-language group read at **162 wpm vs. 222 wpm** for L1 English users (Norwegian vs. English groups, p<.001), at equal comprehension.
— Busby & Dahl (2021), *Nordic Journal of English Studies*, 20(1), pp. 37–50, §1.1 and Table 4.

**VERBATIM (processing/working-memory mechanism):** "adult L2 learners process the L2 in a noisier cognitive architecture … parsing a less used L2 places larger demands on a limited capacity parser so that restrictions in working memory, slower processing speed, and a reduced ability to integrate different information types in real time may compromise the ability of L2 learners to effect complete parses."
— González Alonso / review, *Annual Review of Linguistics* (2022), "Second Language Sentence Processing," §2.

**VERBATIM (speededness disproportionately hits L2 on reading; adult/high-stakes):** "Even for native speakers of English, GRE-V is speeded … But in these reading comprehension items, non-native speakers seemed to have even greater difficulty in completing the test than did the native speakers."
— Angoff & Sharon / ETS (1979), "The Performance of Non-Native Speakers of English on TOEFL and Verbal Aptitude Tests," ETS Research Report, §on speededness. Corroborated by the accommodation metas: "effect sizes [were] larger for studies with extra time or untimed conditions" (Pennock-Román & Rivera 2011).

**VERBATIM (time-limit interaction, recent adult quasi-high-stakes):** native speakers outscore non-natives; "A SJT with video-animated stimuli and a media-supported response format can reduce subgroup differences … between native and non-native speakers." (i.e., removing text load shrinks the L1–L2 gap.)
— Grebner et al. (2024), *BMC Medical Education*, 24:558, abstract.

**Methodology:** Busby & Dahl = timed academic reading + comprehension, N=295 undergrads, 1,415-word text. Annual Review = narrative synthesis of L2 sentence-processing experiments (self-paced reading, eye-tracking). ETS 1979 = classic differential-speededness analysis on GRE-V/TOEFL. BMC 2024 = experimental manipulation of stimulus modality × language proficiency × time-limit awareness in a quasi-high-stakes med-school SJT.

**GRADE: B+.** Converging evidence from multiple independent designs (reading-rate studies, sentence-processing experiments, differential-speededness analyses, a modality-manipulation experiment) that **L2 readers are reliably slower (~25% more time) and are disproportionately penalized under time pressure**, at equal comprehension. Adult/university/high-stakes populations are represented (rare and rewarded). Capped below A because: the "~25% more time" figure is advanced-immersed L2 (Busby & Dahl found NO L1–L2 gap for immersed advanced users — proficiency-dependent), and no single RCT isolates "train exam-English → faster reading under CFA time pressure."

## TASK B — MARKET / DRAW (sourced)

**CFA candidate volume is heavily Asian and non-native-English-heavy:**

- **CFA Institute Annual Report FY2025** (official): CFA Program exam administrations — **Global 200,000; APAC 107,400; AMER 50,200; EMEA 42,400.** → **APAC ≈ 54% of all administrations.** FY2024: Global 208,300 / APAC 117,600 (≈56%). FY2022 peak: Global 262,400 / APAC 145,300 (≈55%).
- **"India now ranks as the top market for new Level I candidates, outpacing China for the first time … China, the US, Canada, and the UK round out our top five markets"** — CFA Institute Annual Report FY2025, p. 9. India ≈ 40,000 candidates/yr and the fastest-growing market.
- Independent aggregation (onlymock.com 10-yr analysis of CFA data): **"Asia Pacific dominates — consistently accounting for 56–59% of all global exam-takers,"** top-5 markets (China, India, US, Canada, UK) ≈ 67% of candidates. Also flags the **charterholder imbalance**: APAC = 56–59% of candidates but only ~22% of charterholders (Americas = ~24% of candidates but ~56% of charterholders) — consistent with a *disproportionate attrition/pass-gap* in Asia.

**Draw verdict:** The addressable segment (non-native-English candidates concentrated in China + India + broader APAC) is not a niche — it is a **majority of the candidate pool and the entire growth story.** The charterholder-vs-candidate imbalance is suggestive (NOT proof) of exactly the language/attrition friction the idea targets. **Market draw = large and defensible.** (Note: "APAC candidate" ≠ "non-native English speaker" 1:1 — India has many English-medium candidates — so treat "non-native English" as a large subset, not the full 54–59%. Still clearly headline-scale.)

## BLUNTNESS — the two questions asked

**Q1: Is "reducing extraneous language load improves non-native exam performance" supported by ≥3 independent A/B pillars, or plausible-but-thin?**

**Plausible-but-thin.** Count the pillars honestly *for the specific intervention claim*:

1. **CLT (A)** — supports the *mechanism* (extraneous load steals WM), but is domain-general instructional-design load, **not** L2 language load. Indirect. ✔ mechanism / �’ direct claim.
2. **Linguistic *simplification* of items (A− meta)** — the most direct test, and it is **NULL** (Kieffer g=0.03 ns; Li & Suen: "no support"). This pillar points *against* the naive "simplify the English" version. ✗
3. **Vocabulary / glossary support (A− meta, but small)** — Kieffer dictionary/glossary g=0.146 (only significant accommodation); Pennock-Román 0.229–0.285 with time; Elleman vocab→comprehension d=0.50 custom but **0.10 ns standardized**. Real but small, measure-dependent, mostly children. ~½ pillar.
4. **Time-pressure × L2 (B+)** — converging, adult-inclusive, robust that L2 is slower and speededness hurts L2 more; extra time / de-texting shrinks the gap. ✔ (this is the strongest *specific* leg.)

So: **one A-tier *mechanism* (CLT), one A− meta that is NULL for simplification, one small-but-real A− vocabulary leg, and one solid B+ time-pressure leg.** That is **NOT ≥3 independent A/B pillars all supporting the headline.** The version that survives is a *narrower, weaker* claim: "vocabulary automaticity + mitigating time-driven reading load can produce a small (~0.1–0.15 SD) improvement for lower-proficiency L2 candidates." The bold "simplify/decode the exam English and scores jump" version is **UNVERIFIED-to-contradicted** at the meta level.

**Q2: Is this genuinely ORTHOGONAL to Diamond #1 (transfer engine), or does it collapse into it?**

**Genuinely orthogonal — this part is a clean YES.** Diamond #1's axis is *memory durability + retrieval-produced transfer* (does the knowledge survive and generalize). This axis is *real-time language-processing load during reading/parsing under time pressure* (can the candidate even extract the problem from the English fast enough to deploy knowledge they already have). Different mechanism (working-memory bandwidth in comprehension vs. long-term retrieval strength), different failure mode (misread/too-slow vs. forgotten/can't-apply), different literature (educational measurement + psycholinguistics vs. cognitive psychology of memory). It does **not** collapse into #1. Bonus: vocabulary/collocation SRS is exactly what FSRS is empirically *best* at, so it converts the engine's documented language-bias weakness into a strength — a real product synergy, not overlap.

## VERDICT

**Independent Diamond #3 candidate (grounded + buildable + big draw): WEAK.**

- **Orthogonal axis? YES** — cleanly distinct mechanism/literature from Diamond #1; does not collapse into it.
- **Big draw? YES** — APAC ≈ 54–59% of all CFA candidates and the sole growth engine (India now #1 for new L1); non-native English is majority-adjacent, not niche.
- **Grounded by ≥3 A/B pillars for the headline intervention? NO** — the most direct meta-analytic test (linguistic *simplification*) is null; only vocabulary/glossary (~0.15 SD, small, mostly children, collapses on standardized measures) and time-pressure interaction (B+) survive, on top of a domain-general A-tier CLT *mechanism*. That is a strong *mechanism* story with a **thin *intervention* story**, extrapolated from K-12 ELL research to adult finance professionals (ecological-validity gap).
- **Buildable?** The strongest-evidenced sub-features are cheap and safe (a high-frequency exam-English / command-word / finance-collocation vocab deck rides FSRS; a reading-under-time-pressure drill is deterministic). No LLM-grading trap. So buildability is fine — but you'd be building the *small-effect, well-evidenced* version (vocabulary automaticity + time-pressure practice), **not** the flashy "AI simplifies the vignette" version, which is both a build risk and evidentially unsupported.

**Recommendation to the pipeline:** Do **not** promote this to a saturated Diamond #3 on current evidence. It is a legitimate **orthogonal supporting layer / differentiator for the Asia market**, best scoped as: **(a) a high-frequency exam-English + command-word + finance-collocation SRS deck** (rides the strongest, cheapest evidence and FSRS's strength), plus **(b) a reading-speed-under-time-pressure diagnostic/drill** (B+ time-pressure leg). Headline the *market* (majority-Asian, growth engine) and the *mechanism* (CLT + L2 speededness), but do **not** claim large score gains from "language-load reduction" — the meta evidence caps that at small and measure-dependent. Keep hunting for Diamond #3 elsewhere (H3's worked-example/faded-scaffolding axis looks more A-tier for a headline).
