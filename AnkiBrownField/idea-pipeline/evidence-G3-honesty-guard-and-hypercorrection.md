# FEATURE 1 — Recognition→retrieval honesty guard

Core claim: force a generative answer before reveal; detect when learners self-grade on *familiarity* rather than *true retrieval* (incl. via latency).

## 1A — Metacognitive illusions / fluency inflates self-rated learning (beyond Koriat & Bjork 2005)

### Finding 1A-1 — Font-size illusion (original)
> "JOLs were influenced by font size, as larger font sizes were given higher JOLs, whereas little relationship was evident between font size and recall. … [the effect] persisted despite experience with multiple study-test sessions, use of a forgetting scale to assess predictions, and explicit warning of participants that font size has little effect on memory performance."
> — **Rhodes & Castel, 2008**, *J. Exp. Psychol.: General*, 137(4), 615–625, abstract (doi:10.1037/a0013684).

**Methodology:** Series of experiments; undergraduates studied single words presented at 18 pt vs 48 pt, gave item-by-item JOLs, then free recall. Perceptual size (a memory-irrelevant cue) reliably inflated predicted memory but not actual recall; disrupting fluency (alternating case) eliminated it. Classic within-subjects lab paradigm on word lists.
**GRADE: B−.** Foundational and heavily replicated, but undergrad word-list lab task with an artificial manipulation (font size) that has zero analogue in a CFA card. Establishes the *mechanism* (fluency ≠ learning), not applied magnitude.

### Finding 1A-2 — Font-size effect meta-analysis (the honest correction)
> "When effect sizes were pooled over all studies, the meta-analysis for JOLs revealed a small-to-moderate font size effect … (g = .38). … The meta-analysis for memory performance revealed that there was a small but highly reliable memory benefit for larger words relative to smaller words (g = .05)."
> — **Chang & Brainerd, 2022**, *Metacognition and Learning*, 17(2), 443–476, Discussion (doi:10.1007/s11409-021-09287-3).

**Methodology:** Meta-analysis of the accumulated font-size literature (JOL analysis k=112 effect sizes; univariate + multivariate meta-regressions of moderators). Confirms fluency inflates JOLs (g=.38) but also shows the "pure illusion" story is overstated — larger fonts also help memory slightly, and dissociation only appears in the very-small-to-intermediate range.
**GRADE: B+.** Strong evidence tier (meta, moderators, publication-bias checks) and it *cuts both ways* — good for bluntness: fluency-based miscalibration is real but smaller and more conditional than folklore claims. Still lab word-lists → ecological validity for exam prep is indirect.

**Sub-verdict (1A):** The general phenomenon "learners confuse fluency/familiarity with learning" is **well-established** (Koriat & Bjork 2005 + Rhodes & Castel 2008 + Chang & Brainerd 2022 meta = independent, replicated). But every canonical demo is a lab word-list with an artificial cue; none directly measures "familiarity-loop self-grading on real study material."

## 1B — Does requiring active generation (vs passive reveal + self-rate) improve outcomes / reduce overconfidence?

### Finding 1B-1 — Generation effect meta-analysis
> "The size of the generation effect across the 86 studies was .40 — a benefit of almost half a standard deviation of generation over reading."
> — **Bertsch, Pesta, Wiscott & McDaniel, 2007**, *Memory & Cognition*, 35(2), 201–210, abstract/Results (doi:10.3758/bf03193441).

**Methodology:** Meta-analysis of 445 effect sizes across 86 studies; compared memory for self-generated vs read material, coding 11 moderators. Robust ~0.40 SD advantage for generating over reading, with large moderator variability (the effect shrinks/reverses under some encoding/test conditions).
**GRADE: B+.** Large, replicated meta establishing that *producing* beats *reading* — directly supports "force a generative answer before reveal." Downgraded from A because most included tasks are lab word/synonym generation, and generation ≠ full free retrieval of exam content.

### Finding 1B-2 — Overt vs covert retrieval meta-analysis (the exact "typed answer vs think-it" question)
> "covert retrieval enhances learning to a small but significant extent (g = 0.23) … The meta-analysis also found that overt retrieval is more effective than covert retrieval (g = 0.17), with the effect size of this additional benefit being moderated by the mode by which covert retrieval is performed."
> — **Yu, Shanks et al., 2025**, *Educational Psychology Review*, 37, art. (doi:10.1007/s10648-025-10024-4), abstract. (Author list beyond first author **UNVERIFIED** from source page.)

**Methodology:** Meta-analysis, 18 studies, 2,560 participants. Covert (mental) retrieval beats restudy (g=0.23); overt production (writing/typing/speaking) adds a further g=0.17 over covert, moderated by corrective feedback and retention interval.
**GRADE: B+.** Recent, directly on-point for the honesty guard's *forced overt answer*. Blunt caveat: the *incremental* benefit of forcing overt production over honest mental retrieval is only **g≈0.17** — real but modest; the big win is retrieval itself, not the typing.

### Finding 1B-3 — Passive restudy inflates confidence while lowering retention (the overconfidence half)
> Already in pool: **Roediger & Karpicke, 2006** (*Psychol. Science*, 17(3), 249–255) — restudy *raised confidence* at short delay while *losing* on delayed retention; and **Koriat & Bjork, 2005** foresight bias (answer-visible inflates JOLs). Grades B+/B− respectively.

**GRADE (the specific claim "generation *reduces overconfidence*"): C / partially UNVERIFIED.** The literature robustly shows passive reveal/restudy *inflates* confidence and that generation *improves outcomes*. But a direct, adult, applied experiment showing "forcing generation *lowers* miscalibration/overconfidence versus passive reveal-then-self-rate" was **not located**. This is an inference, not a demonstrated effect — flag it.

**Sub-verdict (1B):** "Generate before reveal beats passive reveal" = **STRONGLY supported** (Bertsch 2007 A−/B+, Yu 2025 B+, plus the whole retrieval-practice pillar already in the pool). "Generation reduces overconfidence specifically" = **thin/inferred**.

## 1C — Can latency/speed detect low-effort "Good" clicks (guessing vs true retrieval)?

### Finding 1C-1 — Latency tracks memory strength, but confidence ≠ latency
> "the overall mean latency for the correctly recalled words was 5966 ms as compared with 13,606 ms for the incorrect recalled words. The difference was significant, F(1, 113) = 114.12 … Thus, recall accuracy and recall latency were consistent as an indicator of a common underlying construct, namely, memory strength." … "subjects' confidence judgment of their own memory to be unaccountable by retrieval fluency (recall latency)."
> — **Jou, 2008**, *Journal of Memory and Language*, 58(4), 1049–1064, Results & abstract (doi:10.1016/j.jml.2007.12.003).

**Methodology:** Three experiments, modified DRM free-recall paradigm; measured per-item recall latency, accuracy, and confidence by output position. Latency and accuracy co-vary as indices of memory strength (correct items ~2.3× faster), but participants' *confidence* was **not** fully explained by latency.
**GRADE: B−.** Clean, replicated demonstration that latency indexes memory strength — supports "slow answers = weaker memory." But undergrad word-list lab; and it explicitly warns latency and subjective confidence dissociate.

### Finding 1C-2 — Confidence is driven by retrieval fluency (drift rate), diffusion-model evidence
> "higher confidence was associated with higher drift rate in all of the six experiments … there was an overall negative effect of non-decision time on confidence."
> — **Hu, Yang & Luo, 2022**, "Retrospective confidence … affected by both retrieval fluency and non-decision time," Results. (Journal/locator **UNVERIFIED** — sourced from author-hosted PDF, metacog.bnu.edu.cn.)

**Methodology:** Six recognition-memory experiments; drift-diffusion decomposition of RT into decision (drift = retrieval fluency) vs non-decision components, regressed onto trial-level confidence.
**GRADE: C+.** Mechanistically supportive (fluency drives confidence, part of it noise) but lab recognition tasks and I could not verify the publication venue → treat as suggestive only.

### The applied claim — "latency can flag dishonest/low-effort *Good* clicks in a flashcard app"
**GRADE: D / UNVERIFIED.** No study tests this. The strength→latency link is real in lab recall, but (a) it was never validated as a *lie/effort detector*, (b) Jou 2008 shows latency and confidence dissociate, and (c) app-level RT is confounded by reading time, distraction, device, and card length. **Do not claim latency detects self-deception as an evidenced feature** — at best it's a heuristic signal requiring its own validation.

## 1D — Are JOLs better calibrated *after* a retrieval attempt?

### Finding 1D-1 — Delayed-JOL meta-analysis (delay forces covert retrieval)
> "delaying JOLs leads to robust benefits to relative accuracy (g = 0.93). … delayed JOLs result in a modest but reliable benefit for memory performance relative to immediate JOLs (g = 0.08)."
> — **Rhodes & Tauber, 2011**, *Psychological Bulletin*, 137(1), 131–148, abstract (doi:10.1037/a0021705).

**Methodology:** Two meta-analyses — relative accuracy (gamma JOL↔memory) across 112 effect sizes / 4,554 participants; memory performance across 98 effect sizes / 3,807 participants. Delaying the judgment (so it requires a retrieval attempt from LTM rather than a fluency read) massively improves *resolution* (which items you know) with only a trivial memory boost.
**GRADE: B+.** Large, replicated; directly supports "judge learning *after* trying to retrieve, not on reveal." Downgraded from A because it is lab word-pair/list dominated, and "delayed JOL" is a proxy for "post-retrieval JOL."

### Finding 1D-2 — Blunt caveat: delay helps *resolution* but *worsens absolute calibration*
> "The calibration accuracy for predicting final recall decreased as the delay between study and JOL increased … In contrast, relative accuracy (as measured by an overall gamma) improved as the delay between study and JOL increased."
> — **Van Overschelde & Nelson, 2006**, *Memory & Cognition*, 34(7), 1527–1538, abstract (doi:10.3758/bf03195916).

**Methodology:** Swahili–English pairs studied, then immediate / ~1 min / ~8 min pre-JOL recall + JOLs, then final recall; PRAM-based analytic separation of absolute (calibration) vs relative (resolution) accuracy.
**GRADE: B.** Important nuance-killer: post-retrieval JOLs get *ordering* right but can become *over-optimistic in absolute terms* (people over-credit items recallable now). So "better calibrated after retrieval" is TRUE for resolution, FALSE-to-mixed for calibration.

**Sub-verdict (1D):** Post-retrieval judgment improves *which-items-you-know* accuracy strongly; it does **not** cleanly fix absolute over/under-confidence. Design the guard around resolution, not a calibrated probability.

---

## FEATURE 1 — VERDICT

**Core (force generative retrieval before reveal): STRONGLY supported.** Independent A/B pillars ≥ 3:
1. Generation effect meta (Bertsch 2007, B+)
2. Overt>covert retrieval meta (Yu 2025, B+)
3. Delayed/post-retrieval JOL meta (Rhodes & Tauber 2011, B+)
4. Fluency-illusion pillar (Chang & Brainerd 2022 meta B+, + Koriat & Bjork / Rhodes & Castel)

**The *novel* half of the feature — "detect familiarity-loop self-grading, especially via latency" — is POORLY supported / partly UNVERIFIED.** Latency↔strength is real in the lab (Jou 2008, B−) but was never validated as an effort/honesty detector; the "reduces overconfidence" and "latency catches lazy Good clicks" claims have **no direct evidence** and are confounded in a real app. **Blunt call: ship the forced-generation + post-retrieval-judgment mechanic on A/B-tier ground; treat any latency-based "you're lying to the algorithm" signal as an unproven heuristic that needs its own validation, not as evidenced science.**

# FEATURE 2 — Confidence × correctness calibration + hypercorrection

Core claim: independent evidence (beyond Butterfield & Metcalfe 2001) that high-confidence errors are corrected most, that confidence-marking works in high-stakes assessment, and that targeting high-confidence errors improves efficiency.

## 2A — Hypercorrection: independent replications & magnitude

### Finding 2A-1 — Independent replication + persistence + the return caveat
> "The hypercorrection effect is a highly replicable phenomenon that has been independently observed in experiments conducted by several different research groups (Butler, Karpicke, & Roediger, 2008; Butterfield & Metcalfe, 2006; Fazio & Marsh, 2009, 2010; Kulhavy, Yekovich, & Dyer, 1976)." … "the mean gamma correlations for the 6-min [M = .28; t(24) = 4.22, SEM = .07, d = 0.86] and 1-week [M = .19; t(24) = 3.95, SEM = .05, d = 0.79] retention groups were each significantly different from zero."
> — **Butler, Fazio & Marsh, 2011**, *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review*, 18, 1238–1244 (doi:10.3758/s13423-011-0173-y).

**Methodology:** Short-answer general-knowledge test → 7-pt confidence → feedback → retest at 6 min or 1 week. Within-subject gamma between initial confidence and later correction of errors. Effect is robust immediately (d=0.86) and persists at a week (d=0.79) — but high-confidence errors that aren't fully consolidated *return* over time.
**GRADE: B.** Independent lab, general-knowledge (better than word pairs), delayed, with effect sizes. Undergrad sample and correlational (gamma) design cap it below A.

### Finding 2A-2 — Authoritative synthesis: robust across labs, populations, delays
> "the high-confidence errors are more likely to be corrected on the retest than are errors endorsed with lower confidence (e.g., Butler et al. 2008, 2011; Butterfield & Mangels 2003; Butterfield & Metcalfe 2001, 2006; Cyr & Anderson 2013; Eich et al. 2013; Fazio & Marsh 2009, 2010; …). … A strong degree of belief in the truth of one's errors makes them more, rather than less, susceptible to being correctable."
> — **Metcalfe, 2017**, "Learning from Errors," *Annual Review of Psychology*, 68, 465–489 (doi:10.1146/annurev-psych-010416-044022).

**Methodology:** Peer-reviewed annual review synthesizing ~15+ independent studies incl. children (Metcalfe & Finn 2012), young and older adults (Eich et al. 2013), ERP work, and delayed retests.
**GRADE: B+.** Broad, independent, multi-population replication → **this decisively upgrades the ledger's "C+ single-paradigm" rating for the *existence* of hypercorrection.** Not A because it remains lab general-knowledge trivia, not high-stakes applied exam data, and effects are gamma correlations.

### Finding 2A-3 — Blunt magnitude/mechanism caveats (why it's thinner than it looks)
> "People make high-confidence errors only rarely." — **Metcalfe, 2017**, *Annu. Rev. Psychol.*, 68, §"Confidence in Errors."
> "These results converge on the conclusion that when people said that they knew it all along, they were right. This knowledge, no doubt, contributes to why they are able to correct those high-confidence errors so easily." — **Metcalfe & Finn, 2011**, *J. Exp. Psychol.: LMC*, 37, 437–448 (doi:10.1037/a0021962).

**Methodology / import:** (a) Base-rate problem — high-confidence errors are *rare*, so a feature built on them addresses a small slice of items. (b) The effect is substantially explained by *prior partial knowledge* ("knew-it-all-along"), i.e., the learner was already semantically close to the answer — it is not evidence that being wrong-and-confident magically boosts learning. (c) Older adults show a **weakened** hypercorrection (Eich et al. 2013), so it is not population-invariant.
**GRADE: B (as caveats).** Well-sourced limits that must temper any product claim.

**Sub-verdict (2A):** Hypercorrection *exists and replicates independently* — **robust, not thin, for existence** (upgrade from C+ → B/B+). BUT magnitude is modest (gamma ~.2–.3), the target events are rare, and the mechanism is largely "prior knowledge," not a special error-boost.

## 2B — Confidence/certainty-based marking in high-stakes assessment

### Finding 2B-1 — Certainty-Based Marking improves reliability of real summative medical exams
> "CBM has been used at UCL in 17 summative medical exams (years 1&2), each with 250-300 True/False questions and >300 students. … [Cronbach Alpha] was higher for CBM scores than for NCOR (92.4% vs 88.7%, difference 3.7±0.31% SEM, n=17, P<0.001%). … The mean correlation coefficient (r) for CBM was 0.859±0.030 SD, significantly greater than for NCOR (0.814±0.030 …)."
> — **Gardner-Medwin & Curtin**, "Analysis of exams using certainty-based marking," *The Physiological Society* (Proc.), abstract.

**Methodology:** Applied deployment across 17 summative exams, >300 medical students each, 3-point certainty scale (marks 1/2/3 correct; penalties 0/−2/−6) — a *proper* scoring rule. Reliability (Cronbach α; odd/even split-half) higher for CBM than number-correct, and CBM predicted number-correct on held-out items better than number-correct predicted itself.
**GRADE: B+ (borderline A− for applied fit).** Genuinely adult, high-stakes, professional-track, large N, repeated — exactly the population type the grading rubric rewards. Held below A because it's essentially one research program (Gardner-Medwin/UCL) and it measures *psychometric reliability*, not *learning gains*.

### Finding 2B-2 — Efficiency of confidence data + independent uptake
> "confidence-based data can give results of equivalent statistical reliability to conventional marking with fewer questions, by a factor of 2.0 for the lowest third of our groups and 2.2 for the top third."
> — **Gardner-Medwin & Gahan, 2003**, "Formative and Summative Confidence-Based Assessment," *Proc. CAA Conf.* (tmedwin.net/~ucgbarg/tea/caa03a.pdf).
> Independent replication: **2024 prospective cohort**, second-year medical students, found CBM "increase[s] the validity of assessments" vs number-right scoring — *Comparison of CBM and Number Right Scoring*, PMC12640580 (2024).

**GRADE: B.** Confidence marking extracts more information per item (≈2× reliability efficiency) and has ≥1 independent modern replication. Still assessment-reliability evidence, adjacent to (not identical with) a *learning* mechanism.

**Sub-verdict (2B):** Confidence/certainty marking is **well-supported as a scoring/measurement method** in real high-stakes adult exams (rare applied win). It is *not* by itself evidence that surfacing confidence *teaches* better.

## 2C — Does targeting high-confidence errors improve *learning efficiency*?

### Finding 2C-1 — The contradicting pillar: optimal effort goes to the "region of proximal learning," not the hardest/most-confident-wrong items
> "the order is from that which they believe is almost known to that which they believe is more difficult (high JOL to low JOL). … [learners] should turn initially to those items that are least difficult, so long as they are not yet learned … Only late in [the] process should they turn to the most [difficult]."
> — **Metcalfe & Kornell, 2005**, "A Region of Proximal Learning model of study time allocation," *J. Memory & Language*, 52, 463–477.

**Methodology:** Model + eight experiments on study-time allocation; efficient learning prioritizes items just beyond current mastery, not the hardest items.
**GRADE: B (as a counter-pillar).** Directly tensions "spend extra resources on confident errors": those confident errors are (per 2A) already the *easiest to fix* and rare, so lavishing scheduling weight on them may be low-yield versus mid-difficulty items.

### Finding 2C-2 — What *is* supported: test the correction immediately to lock it in
> Metcalfe & Miele (2014), *J. Appl. Res. Mem. Cogn.*, 3, 189–197 — "Prior testing both enhances delayed performance and blocks the return of the errors" (cited in Metcalfe 2017 reference list; primary abstract **not independently fetched → treat locator as verified, effect size UNVERIFIED**).

**GRADE: C+.** Supports "after a confident miss, immediately re-test the correction" — a sound design nudge — but this is about *feedback timing*, not proof that *prioritizing* high-confidence errors beats normal spacing on efficiency.

### The applied claim — "targeting high-confidence errors improves learning efficiency"
**GRADE: C / largely UNVERIFIED.** No located study shows that *allocating extra study/scheduling to high-confidence errors* yields more learning-per-minute than standard difficulty-based scheduling. Hypercorrection says these errors are corrected *readily anyway* (so marginal ROI of extra targeting may be *low*), and RPL evidence says effort is better spent on near-mastery items. **Blunt: surfacing confident errors is a good UX/attention nudge with real feedback-timing support, but "targets them for efficiency gains" is an assumption, not an evidenced result.**

---

## FEATURE 2 — VERDICT

**Hypercorrection existence: robustly / STRONGLY supported** — and the ledger's "C+ single-paradigm, thin" tag is **outdated**. Independent A/B-ish pillars ≥ 3:
1. Butler, Fazio & Marsh 2011 (independent replication + persistence, B)
2. Metcalfe 2017 Annual Review (multi-lab, multi-population synthesis, B+)
3. Metcalfe & Finn 2011 (mechanism / independent, B)
4. (adjacent) Gardner-Medwin CBM applied reliability (B+)

**BUT the *product-relevant* claims are weaker:**
- Magnitude is modest (gamma ~.2–.3), high-confidence errors are **rare**, effect is largely **prior-knowledge driven**, and **attenuated in older adults**.
- Confidence *marking* is a strong *measurement* method (adult, high-stakes) — **not** demonstrated to *improve learning*.
- "Target high-confidence errors for efficiency" is **contradicted** by the Region-of-Proximal-Learning allocation evidence and **unproven** directly.

**Net:** Feature 2's headline ("confident errors are correctable / worth surfacing") clears ≥3 independent B/B+ pillars — **promote it out of "thin."** Its *efficiency* and *learning-lift* sub-claims do **not** clear the bar; they are TENTATIVE-to-POOR and lab-bound. Build the surfacing + immediate-recorrection loop; do **not** market a "we make you learn faster by hunting confident errors" claim without new evidence.
